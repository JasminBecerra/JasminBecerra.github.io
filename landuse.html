Below is an overview of a weeks-long lab assignment I completed for my first GIS course.
Jasmin Becerra, Spring 2016

Over the course of about 5 weeks, students were tasked with finding the most suitable sites for a hypothetical development project involving the construction of a small lab and office building.

These were the given parameters:

1. A local ordinance designed to prevent rampant development only allows new construction within 300 meters of existing sewer lines.

2. A recent national water quality act requires that NO construction occur within 20 meters of streams.

3. The site should not be forested (eliminating the cost of clearing land). And, because a regional agricultural preservation plan prohibits converting farmland in this area, the current land use must be brush land.

4. The site must overlie soils suitable for buildings.

5. The site must be at least 2,000 square meters in size.

To get started, I georeference a scanned map of our study area. Then, I digitized (on-screen method) the georeferenced map in order to create digital polygons and lines. From there, I edited attributes and feature and saved them as files into a geodatabase. Then I symbolized features: rivers became blue line features, land types were color coded, etc.

At that point, the map was ready for land use analysis.

I created buffers per the paramaters for sewers (300m within) and streams (20m away) and erased/subtracted the stream buffer from the sewer buffer.

Then I selected the brushland from the land use polygons, and selected suitable soil and intersected both polygons.

I intersected what remained of the sewer buffer with the suitable brushland layer, which resulted in several polygons. Then, I selected only the polygons that had an area of at least 2000 square meters. I was left with 14 suitable locations for the hypothetical project.
